Welcome to this investment analysis report. This has been prepared for a private investor in Finland who has one hundred and twenty thousand euros in cash and wants to buy a small apartment, either a studio or a two-room flat, in Helsinki. The plan is straightforward but quite clever. First, buy the flat and rent it out for five to ten years, earning rental income the whole time. Then, when the investor's child eventually moves to Helsinki for university or work, the child lives in the flat. And later, when the child no longer needs it, they sell it, ideally completely tax-free. We will explain exactly how all of this works over the next thirty minutes or so.

Let us start with the big picture of Helsinki's housing market, because the timing of this purchase matters enormously.

Helsinki apartment prices have been falling continuously for three and a half years now, since the summer of two thousand and twenty-two. This is the longest unbroken price decline since nineteen forty-seven, which is essentially the entire period for which Finland has reliable housing data. From the peak to now, prices have dropped about fourteen percent on average. In two thousand and twenty-five, Helsinki apartment prices fell by about two percent overall. The most authoritative forecasters, an institution called PTT, which stands for Pellervon taloustutkimus, and another called Hypo, which is Finland's mortgage guarantee institution, both predict that prices will continue falling slightly in two thousand and twenty-six, by somewhere between one and one point four percent. However, both also expect a recovery to begin in two thousand and twenty-seven, with Helsinki region prices rising by about three percent.

What does this mean for our investor? It means that two thousand and twenty-six is a near-optimal buying window. Prices are close to their cyclical bottom, and the market is already showing signs of life with transaction volumes rising. But let us be honest, there is always uncertainty. Geopolitical risks, including the situations in Iran and Ukraine and trade disputes, could delay the recovery. But for a buyer with a five to ten year horizon and no need for a mortgage, these short-term fluctuations matter far less.

Now, let us talk about what one hundred and twenty thousand euros can actually buy in Helsinki.

Helsinki is a city of neighborhoods, and prices vary enormously depending on where you look. The citywide average price for an older apartment is around four thousand nine hundred euros per square meter. But in the eastern suburbs along the metro line, prices can be as low as one thousand eight hundred to two thousand six hundred euros per square meter. This means our investor has a remarkably wide selection. Twenty-five neighborhoods in Helsinki proper have average prices that allow purchasing a twenty-five square meter studio within the budget. In the cheapest metro-connected areas, like Myllypuro or Kontula, the budget could even stretch to a two-room apartment of forty square meters, with money left over.

Let us now walk through the neighborhoods that emerged as the strongest candidates, starting from the very top.

The number one recommendation is a neighborhood called Myllypuro, which has the postal code zero zero nine two zero. Myllypuro is in eastern Helsinki, directly on the metro line, about fifteen to sixteen minutes from the city center. The key attraction is that it is immediately adjacent to the Metropolia University of Applied Sciences campus, which means there are literally thousands of students looking for housing within walking distance. The average price here is around two thousand six hundred to two thousand eight hundred euros per square meter for completed sales, which means a twenty-five square meter studio costs roughly sixty-five thousand euros. This is extraordinary because it leaves the investor with about fifty-five thousand euros in reserve after buying. That reserve provides an enormous safety buffer.

Myllypuro has undergone significant urban renewal over the past decade. It used to have a rougher reputation, but the area has been transformed with new buildings, improved streets, and better public spaces. A major Finnish newspaper described the transformation as one where everyone won. The building stock here is a mix of nineteen sixties to eighties concrete apartment blocks and newer constructions. For the older buildings, something called a putkiremontti is a critical consideration, which we will discuss in detail shortly.

The second strongest recommendation is a neighborhood called Viikki, postal code zero zero seven nine zero. Viikki is home to the University of Helsinki's Viikki campus, which houses the biological sciences, agriculture, pharmacy, and veterinary science departments, with about five thousand students. The area was largely built in the nineteen nineties to two thousand and tens as a modern eco-district called Latokartano, which means the building stock is relatively new and well-maintained. The average price here is around four thousand three hundred and fifty nine euros per square meter, meaning a twenty-five square meter studio costs approximately one hundred and nine thousand euros. That is within budget but with less reserve than Myllypuro. The big advantage of Viikki is much lower renovation risk because the buildings are newer. The Viikki arboretum and nature reserve are right next door, making it a beautiful and quiet place to live. The downside is that Viikki lacks a metro station, relying on bus connections that take about twenty-five minutes to the city center. However, a new light rail line called Raide-Jokeri is planned to significantly improve connectivity in the coming years.

The third recommendation is Pihlajamäki, postal code zero zero seven one zero, which is located close to the Viikki university campus and can be reached by bus in about ten minutes. Pihlajamäki is a fascinating neighborhood. It was built primarily in the nineteen sixties as an architecturally significant forest suburb, and it is actually protected as an architectural heritage area. Prices here are very affordable, at around two thousand six hundred and fifty euros per square meter, meaning a studio costs about sixty-six thousand euros. The challenge is that many of the older buildings will need or are currently undergoing that major renovation we keep mentioning, the putkiremontti. A buyer needs to be very selective about which specific building they choose.

Other noteworthy neighborhoods include Länsi-Pasila, which is right next to Finland's busiest train station and walkable to the Haaga-Helia University of Applied Sciences campus. Pohjois-Haaga, another neighborhood near the Haaga-Helia campus, offers good value at around ninety-nine thousand euros for a twenty-five square meter studio. And Länsi-Herttoniemi, one metro stop from Myllypuro, is one of Helsinki's most pleasant residential suburbs with leafy streets and proximity to the waterfront.

Now let us talk about what it actually costs to own an apartment in Finland, because the purchase price is only part of the equation.

Every apartment owner in Finland pays something called a vastike, which is a monthly maintenance charge to the housing company, known in Finnish as a taloyhtiö. The housing company owns the building, and apartment owners actually own shares in this company, not the physical walls themselves. This is important to understand because it means the taloyhtiö makes collective decisions about building maintenance, renovations, and finances.

The vastike has at least two components. The first is called hoitovastike, which covers the building's operating costs like heating, water, cleaning, insurance, minor repairs, and building management. The national average for hoitovastike was four euros and eighty-six cents per square meter per month in two thousand and twenty-four, according to Finland's official statistics bureau, Tilastokeskus. In the greater Helsinki area, it was slightly higher at five euros and twenty-two cents per square meter per month. For older buildings from the nineteen sixties to eighties, it typically ranges from four to five and a half euros per square meter. For a twenty-five square meter studio in an older building, that means roughly one hundred and twenty-five euros per month.

The second component is called rahoitusvastike, which means financing charge. This exists when the taloyhtiö has taken out a loan, called yhtiölaina. It is essentially the share of the building's communal loan that each apartment must repay. In new construction, the rahoitusvastike can be several hundred euros per month, which dramatically changes the economics of ownership. We will come back to this.

Adding up all the monthly costs for a typical older studio apartment, you are looking at about one hundred and fifty euros per month including the maintenance charge, water costs, and landlord's insurance. For a newer apartment with a building loan, this can balloon to four hundred to six hundred euros per month. The difference is enormous and directly affects your investment returns.

Let us now turn to a concept that may be unfamiliar but is absolutely crucial for anyone buying an apartment in Finland. It is called putkiremontti, or in English, a pipe renovation, though the formal term is linjasaneeraus.

Every building has a lifespan for its plumbing, including both water supply pipes and drainage pipes. In Finland, this lifespan is typically forty to sixty years. Buildings constructed in the nineteen sixties and seventies are now at or past this age, which means their pipes need replacing. A putkiremontti is one of the most expensive renovations a building can undergo. According to a Finnish renovation consulting firm called Sustera, the average cost of a full traditional putkiremontti in greater Helsinki is about fifty thousand euros per apartment. The cost per square meter ranges from five hundred to one thousand five hundred euros, depending on the scope of work.

Here is a critical detail that many first-time buyers miss: the cost of a putkiremontti is largely determined by the number of bathrooms, not by the apartment's size. A twenty-five square meter studio with one bathroom costs nearly the same to renovate as a sixty square meter apartment with one bathroom. This means that studios bear a proportionally higher putkiremontti cost relative to their value.

The putkiremontti usually includes replacing all water and drainage pipes, and it is often combined with renovating the bathrooms, upgrading the heating and ventilation systems, and updating the electrical wiring. During the renovation, residents typically need to vacate their apartment for two to five months while their section of the building is worked on.

For our investor, this is perhaps the single most important risk factor to consider. The recommended strategy is to buy a building where the putkiremontti has already been completed. This eliminates the largest financial risk entirely. A building that completed its pipe renovation a few years ago will be priced slightly higher than one where the renovation is still pending, but it means no surprise fifty-thousand-euro bill arriving in the future. Post-renovation buildings also tend to have newer, more attractive bathrooms, which helps with rental demand.

If the investor prefers the lower purchase price of a pre-renovation building, they should ensure they have at least thirty to fifty thousand euros set aside in reserve. This is why the Myllypuro option is so attractive: buying a studio there for sixty-five thousand euros leaves roughly fifty-five thousand euros in reserve, which is more than enough to cover even a full putkiremontti if it arises.

Now let us discuss the rental income side of the equation, because this is how the investment generates returns while waiting for the child to grow up.

In Helsinki, the average rental price for a studio apartment is twenty-five euros per square meter per month, according to data from late twenty twenty-five. For a twenty-five square meter studio, that translates to about six hundred and twenty-five euros per month. In the eastern suburbs where we are primarily looking, rents are somewhat lower, typically ranging from five hundred to five hundred and sixty euros per month for a studio.

How much of this rent actually ends up in the investor's pocket? Let us walk through a detailed example using the top recommendation, a twenty-five square meter studio in Myllypuro purchased for sixty-five thousand euros.

Monthly rental income would be approximately five hundred and sixty-three euros. From this, we subtract the hoitovastike of about one hundred and twenty-five euros, water charges of about fifteen euros, and a share of the annual insurance premium at about ten euros. That leaves a gross cash flow of four hundred and thirteen euros per month. After setting aside a vacancy reserve equivalent to one month's rent per year, which accounts for the possibility of the apartment sitting empty between tenants, the net monthly cash flow is about three hundred and sixty-six euros.

But of course, the government takes its share. In Finland, rental income is classified as pääomatuloa, which means capital income, and it is taxed at thirty percent on the first thirty thousand euros and thirty-four percent above that. The investor can deduct various expenses from the rental income before calculating tax, including the entire hoitovastike, water charges, landlord's insurance, travel costs for property inspections, and renovation costs. After paying thirty percent tax on the net rental income, the after-tax monthly income is approximately two hundred and eighty-nine euros. This gives an after-tax annual yield of about five point three percent on the invested capital.

Let us put this number in context. Five point three percent after tax on a zero-leverage, cash-purchased asset is quite good. It compares favorably with, for example, Finnish government bonds, savings accounts, or even many stock market dividend yields. And it comes with the added benefit of a physical asset that the child will eventually live in.

There is a critical tax detail that every aspiring landlord in Finland needs to understand about something called rahoitusvastike, the financing charge we mentioned earlier. Its tax treatment depends on how the housing company records it in its accounts. If it is recorded as income, which is called tuloutettu in Finnish, the payment is fully deductible from your rental income each year, which reduces your annual tax bill. If it is recorded as capital, which is called rahastoitu, it is not deductible from rent but instead gets added to your acquisition cost, which reduces your tax bill when you eventually sell. Most new buildings use the income method, which benefits investors during the holding period. This is one of the reasons some investment guides promote buying new apartments with heavy yhtiölaina, although we do not recommend that approach for this particular investor because of the associated risks.

Speaking of selling, let us now discuss the most brilliant part of this entire strategy, the tax-free exit.

In Finland, there is a very generous tax exemption for selling your own home, known as oman asunnon verovapaus. If you have owned the property for at least two years and either you or a family member, such as a spouse or minor child, has lived in it as their permanent home continuously for at least two years during your ownership period, then the entire capital gain from the sale is completely tax-free.

So here is how the strategy works. The parent buys the apartment in twenty twenty-six. For the next five to ten years, it is rented out, generating about five percent annual return. The rental income is taxed normally during this period. Then the child moves to Helsinki for university or work and lives in the apartment as their primary residence for at least two continuous years. After those two years, if they decide to move or if the family decides to sell, the entire sale proceeds, minus the original purchase price, are completely free from capital gains tax. Given that Helsinki prices are expected to recover and grow over the five to ten year holding period, this could represent a significant tax saving.

To illustrate with numbers: suppose the apartment is bought for sixty-five thousand euros in twenty twenty-six, and after ten years of price recovery, it is worth ninety thousand euros. If sold as an investment property, the capital gain of approximately twenty-three thousand euros after adjustments would be taxed at thirty percent, resulting in a tax bill of about seven thousand euros. But if the child has lived there for two years first, that entire gain is tax-free. Seven thousand euros saved, simply by having the child live there before selling.

Let us now address the overall cost of buying and the step-by-step process.

When you buy an apartment in Finland, the main transaction cost is the transfer tax, called varainsiirtovero. For housing company shares, which is what apartment ownership technically is, the rate is one point five percent of the purchase price. Importantly, this is calculated only on the actual cash you pay, called the kauppahinta, not on the velaton hinta which would include any housing company loan. So for a sixty-five thousand euro purchase, the transfer tax is nine hundred and seventy-five euros. That is the only significant transaction cost. There is no buyer's agent fee in Finland as the seller pays the agent, and legal fees are generally not needed for straightforward apartment purchases.

It is worth noting that Finland removed the first-time buyer transfer tax exemption in twenty twenty-four, so this tax now applies to everyone regardless of whether it is their first or tenth apartment purchase.

After the purchase, the buyer must register the ownership change in Finland's digital housing registry, called the Huoneistotietojärjestelmä, which is managed by the National Land Survey, Maanmittauslaitos. The registration fee is eighty-nine euros. The buyer must also file the transfer tax declaration through the electronic tax service called OmaVero within two months of the purchase.

Before buying, there is one document that is absolutely essential to review. It is called the isännöitsijäntodistus, which translates as the building manager's certificate. This document contains everything you need to know about the housing company and the specific apartment. It tells you the current monthly charges, the total amount of any housing company loan allocated to the apartment, the building's renovation history, and most importantly, the planned future renovations for the next five to ten years, including estimated costs. If the putkiremontti is listed as upcoming or under planning, you know exactly what you are getting into. The isännöitsijäntodistus also tells you about the taloyhtiö's financial health, including its reserve fund and any debts.

Now let us discuss a category of apartments that show up very prominently when searching online but that our analysis recommends avoiding for this particular investor. These are what we call category B listings, apartments with very low selling prices but massive housing company loans.

When browsing Helsinki apartment listings, you will see studios for sale at prices like twenty-five thousand or thirty thousand euros. These are almost always newer buildings, built in the late twenty-tens or twenty twenties, where the developer financed the construction with a large housing company loan. The actual debt-free price of these apartments may be one hundred and fifty to two hundred and fifty thousand euros, far above our budget when you account for the loan. The buyer pays only the low selling price in cash, but then becomes responsible for repaying the building's loan share through monthly rahoitusvastike payments of several hundred euros.

For our investor who has one hundred and twenty thousand euros in cash and wants to minimize risk and maximize control, these apartments present several problems. The monthly costs are very high, sometimes eating up most or all of the rental income. The apartments can be difficult to sell later because future buyers must also accept the large loan burden. And in the worst case, if the housing company faces financial difficulties or interest rates rise sharply, the rahoitusvastike can increase unpredictably. Since twenty twenty-five, new regulations cap the housing company loan at sixty percent of the debt-free price, which reduces the most extreme cases but does not eliminate the risks entirely.

The recommendation is clear. With one hundred and twenty thousand euros in cash, the investor should buy a standard older apartment with minimal or no housing company loan, paying the full price upfront and keeping monthly costs very low. This is the conservative, low-risk approach that provides the best combination of yield, safety, and flexibility.

First, always check the building's renovation plan before buying. The isännöitsijäntodistus is your best friend. If a putkiremontti is listed within the next five years and you are buying to hold for five to ten years, you will be hit with a major cost during your ownership period.

Second, the Helsinki rental market is currently in the tenant's favor, meaning there are more apartments available than people looking to rent. Starting rents have been declining slightly. Budget conservatively for rent and assume the apartment might sit empty for one month per year between tenants.

Third, market forecasts are just forecasts. While both major institutional sources agree that prices will decline slightly in twenty twenty-six and recover from twenty twenty-seven, there is no guarantee. External shocks, rising interest rates, or an economic downturn could extend the price decline.

Fourth, tax laws can change. The capital gains exemption for own-residence sales has existed for decades and is politically popular, but future governments could modify the rules. The same applies to the deductibility of expenses from rental income.

Fifth, the child's needs should be considered. A studio is a good starting apartment for a university student, but if they are studying at a university that is far from the chosen neighborhood, the property becomes less useful as a future home and remains purely an investment. The metro line connects Myllypuro conveniently to many parts of Helsinki, making it versatile, but Viikki is best if the child studies biology, agriculture, or pharmacy at the Viikki campus.

Despite these caveats, the fundamental case remains strong. Helsinki's population grew by eleven thousand five hundred in twenty twenty-five, the strongest growth since the nineteen sixties. Construction of new apartments has dropped to historic lows. These two forces, growing demand and shrinking supply, will support prices over the medium and long term. A cash buyer purchasing near the bottom of a three and a half year decline, in an area with structural tenant demand, is positioning themselves well for both income and eventual capital appreciation.
