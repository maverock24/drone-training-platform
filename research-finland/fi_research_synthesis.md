## Section 1: Finnish Capital Income Tax Structure (Pääomatulovero) — Objective 1

### 1.1 Capital Income Tax Rates (Pääomatuloveron verokannat) — 2026

Finland taxes capital income (pääomatulo) separately from earned income (ansiotulo) using a two-tier progressive structure:

| Capital Income Band | Tax Rate | Source |
|---------------------|----------|--------|
| Up to €30,000 | **30%** | [1][2] |
| Exceeding €30,000 | **34%** | [1][2] |

These rates apply to all capital income types: capital gains (luovutusvoitto), rental income (vuokratulo), dividend income (osinkotulo), interest income (korkotulo), life insurance yields, and capital income share of business income ([Vero.fi — Pääomatulot](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/verokortti-ja-veroilmoitus/tulot/paaomatulot/)).

**Example at €40,000 gross earned income with €35,000 capital income:**
- First €30,000 × 30% = €9,000
- Remaining €5,000 × 34% = €1,700
- **Total capital income tax: €10,700** (effective rate: 30.6%)

### 1.2 Key 2026 Changes Affecting Capital Income

Per [Vero.fi — What will change in taxation in 2026](https://www.vero.fi/en/About-us/newsroom/news/uutiset/2025/what-will-change-in-taxation-in-2026/) (8 Dec 2025):

- **Bank loyalty bonuses** now taxed as capital income — discounts and benefits from banks based on customer relationships, loans, or investment products are subject to pääomatulovero starting 2026.

- **Forestry deduction** increased to 75% of capital income (from 60%).
- **No change** to the 30%/34% capital income tax rate structure or the €30,000 threshold for 2026.

### 1.4 Alijäämähyvitys (Deficit Credit) — Capital Loss → Earned Income Tax Relief

When deductions from capital income exceed actual capital income, a **deficit** (alijäämä) arises. The Tax Administration automatically converts part of this deficit into a credit against earned income taxes:

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Credit rate | **30%** of the deficit amount | [5] |
| Annual maximum | **€1,400** per person | [5] |
| Child increase — 1 child | +€400 → max **€1,800** | [5] |
| Child increase — 2+ children | +€800 → max **€2,200** | [5] |
| Spousal transfer | Unused max can transfer to spouse (automatic) | [5] |
| Unused deficit beyond max | Confirmed as capital income category loss (pääomatulolajin tappio), carried forward **10 years** | [5] |

**Practical example from Vero.fi:** Matti has €11,000 capital income deficit and €15,000 in earned income taxes. 30% × €11,000 = €3,300, but capped by his available maximum. With spouse Maija (no deficit, unused €1,400 max transfers to Matti) + 1 child (+€400): Matti's effective cap = €1,400 + €1,400 + €400 = **€3,200**. His earned income taxes reduce from €15,000 to €11,800.

**Relevance for middle-income investors:** If investment loan interest or capital losses create a deficit, the alijäämähyvitys mechanism provides meaningful tax relief (up to €1,400–€2,200 reduced from ansiotulovero), making it an important tool for tax planning.

## Section 2: Osakesäästötili (OST) — Equity Savings Account — Objective 2

### 2.1 Overview

The osakesäästötili (OST) is Finland's tax-advantaged equity investment account, introduced in 2020 and available to all Finnish tax residents. It offers **tax deferral** on all trading activity and dividends within the account — tax is only triggered upon withdrawal.

### 2.2 Key Rules (2026)

| Rule | Detail | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Deposit ceiling** | **€100,000** total deposits (raised from €50,000 on 1.1.2024) | [6][7] |
| **Allowed instruments** | **Listed shares only** (Finnish and foreign stock exchange). NOT mutual funds, ETFs via fund structure, bonds, or unlisted shares | [6] |
| **Deposits** | Cash only — you cannot transfer existing shares into the account | [6] |
| **Number of accounts** | **One** OST per person. €10/day penalty for holding multiple simultaneously | [6] |
| **Tax on internal trades** | **None** — buy/sell freely within the account | [6] |
| **Dividends inside account** | **Not taxed** until withdrawal — can be reinvested tax-free | [6] |
| **Withdrawal taxation** | Proportional: (gain ÷ total fair value) × withdrawal amount = taxable income | [6] |
| **Tax rate on withdrawal** | 30% up to €30,000 / 34% above €30,000 (standard pääomatulovero) | [6] |
| **Withholding tax** | Bank automatically deducts 30% ennakonpidätys on taxable portion of withdrawal | [6] |
| **Transferable** | Can transfer to another bank without selling shares or triggering tax | [6] |
| **Loss deduction** | Only when **closing** the account entirely — NOT on individual withdrawals | [6] |

### 2.3 Tax Disadvantages vs. Regular Arvo-osuustili

| Feature | OST | Arvo-osuustili |
|---------|-----|----------------|
| **Dividend partial exemption** | ❌ Not available — all gains taxed | ✅ 15% of listed Finnish dividends tax-free |
| **Hankintameno-olettama** | ❌ Not available | ✅ 20% (held <10yr) or 40% (held ≥10yr) acquisition cost presumption |
| **Tax-free small sales** | ❌ Not available | ✅ €1,000/year total sales proceeds tax-free |
| **Loss deduction timing** | Only when closing account | Immediately in the selling year, 5yr carry-forward |

### 2.4 Worked Example (from Vero.fi)

**Sari deposits €10,000 → buys shares → value rises to €15,000 (€5,000 gain).**

Withdrawal of €1,500:
- Taxable portion: (€5,000 ÷ €15,000) × €1,500 = **€500** taxable capital income
- Tax-free return of capital: €1,500 − €500 = **€1,000**
- Remaining deposit balance: €10,000 − €1,000 = **€9,000**
- Tax on €500: €500 × 30% = **€150**

### 2.5 When is OST Most Advantageous?

The OST is most beneficial when:
- **Long holding periods** with frequent rebalancing between stocks (no tax drag on internal trades)
- **Reinvesting dividends** without immediate tax leakage
- **Capital gains are moderate** relative to total account value (proportional withdrawal taxation favorable)
- **Holding period < 10 years** (hankintameno-olettama 40% benefit not available on arvo-osuustili either)

The OST is **less** advantageous when:
- You primarily hold high-dividend Finnish stocks (lose the 15% exemption)
- You plan to hold individual stocks for 10+ years (lose hankintameno-olettama 40%)
- Annual sales would be under €1,000 (lose the tax-free threshold)

## Section 3: Arvo-osuustili — Regular Securities Account Taxation — Objective 3

### 3.1 Capital Gains Tax (Luovutusvoittovero) Calculation

When selling securities from an arvo-osuustili (book-entry account), the capital gain is calculated as:

**Gain = Sale price − (Purchase price + acquisition costs)** OR **Gain = Sale price − Hankintameno-olettama**

The taxpayer chooses whichever method produces the **lower taxable gain** ([Vero.fi — Osakkeiden myynti](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/osakkeiden_myynt/)).

### 3.2 Hankintameno-olettama (Acquisition Cost Presumption)

| Holding Period | Presumption | Calculation |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **< 10 years** | **20%** of sale price | Taxable gain = 80% of sale price |
| **≥ 10 years** | **40%** of sale price | Taxable gain = 60% of sale price |

**Key rules:**
- Cannot combine actual costs with the presumption — choose one or the other
- Includes all acquisition costs (brokerage, transfer tax) in the comparison
- Applies to shares, fund units, and other securities

**Worked example (from Vero.fi):** Antti bought 1,000 shares in 1994 for €679.47 total (incl. fees). Sells in 2025 for €2,500. Held ≥10 years → hankintameno-olettama = 40% × €2,500 = **€1,000**. Actual cost = €679.47 + €25 fees = €704.47. Since presumption (€1,000) > actual cost (€704.47), use presumption → **Taxable gain = €2,500 − €1,000 = €1,500**.

### 3.3 Tax-Free Small Sales Threshold

If **total sale proceeds across all asset sales** (not just shares) during a calendar year are **≤ €1,000**, the gains are completely **tax-free** ([Vero.fi — Osakkeiden myynti](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/osakkeiden_myynt/)).

**Important:** This €1,000 limit applies to total proceeds (myyntihinta), not to the gain. All sales (shares, fund units, other assets) count toward the limit.

### 3.4 FIFO Principle (First In, First Out)

Sales from an arvo-osuustili **must follow FIFO**: oldest shares of the same company are sold first. However, FIFO is **per-account** — if you hold the same company's shares in multiple accounts at different brokers, you can choose which account to sell from ([Vero.fi — Osakkeiden myynti](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/osakkeiden_myynt/)).

### 3.5 Capital Loss Deduction (Luovutustappio)

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Deduction priority** | First from capital gains, then from all capital income |
| **Carry-forward period** | **5 years** |
| **Small sales exclusion** | If total purchase price of sold shares ≤ €1,000/year, losses **cannot** be deducted |
| **Alijäämähyvitys** | Capital losses do **NOT** qualify for alijäämähyvitys — they cannot reduce earned income taxes |

### 3.6 Tax Rate

Standard pääomatulovero: **30%** up to €30,000, **34%** above €30,000. Same as all capital income.

## Section 4: Sijoitusvakuutus — Investment Insurance Wrapper — Objective 4

### 4.1 What is a Sijoitusvakuutus?

A sijoitusvakuutus (investment insurance / savings life insurance) is a **unit-linked insurance wrapper** (vakuutuskuori) offered by Finnish insurance companies. It functions as a tax-deferral vehicle: all trading, fund switches, and dividend income within the wrapper are **tax-free**. Tax is only triggered when withdrawing funds.

### 4.2 Tax Treatment

| Event | Tax Treatment | Source |
|-------|--------------|--------|
| **Internal fund switches** | Tax-free — no capital gains triggered | [11] |
| **Dividends/returns inside wrapper** | Not taxed — compound growth benefit | [11] |
| **Withdrawal (partial or full)** | Return portion (tuotto) taxed as **pääomatulo** (30%/34%). Capital (pääoma) portion tax-free | [11] |
| **Policy maturity to close relative** | Capital subject to **gift tax** (lahjavero). Returns taxed as capital income to recipient | [11] |
| **Death benefit to close relative** | Subject to **inheritance tax** (perintövero) | [11] |
| **Death benefit to non-relative** | Entire amount taxed as **capital income** to recipient | [11] |
| **Losses** | Deductible only when policy terminates in loss, in the termination year | [11] |

### 4.3 Fee Structure (Typical)

Per [Sijoittaja.fi — Vakuutuskuoret](https://www.sijoittaja.fi/60607/vakuutuskuoret-kannattaako-kapitalisaatiosopimus-tai-sijoitusvakuutus/) (May 2025):

| Fee Type | Typical Range | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| **Management fee (hoitopalkkio)** | 0.3%–1.0% p.a. of portfolio value | Varies by provider and portfolio size |
| **Underlying fund fees** | 0.2%–2.0% p.a. | On top of wrapper management fee |
| **Surrender charge (takaisinostomaksu)** | 0%–3% | Often decreases with holding period; some providers have no charge |
| **Entry fee (aloitusmaksu)** | €0–€500 | One-time; negotiable for larger amounts |
| **Fund switch fee (vaihtokulu)** | Usually free or €0–€30 | Most providers offer free switches |

**Key providers:** Mandatum (formerly Mandatum Life), OP Henkivakuutus, Nordea Henkivakuutus, LähiTapiola, Fennia

### 4.4 Advantages vs. Disadvantages

**Advantages:**
- Tax deferral on internal trades → compound growth benefit (korkoa korolle)
- Can switch between different funds/asset classes without triggering tax
- Estate planning: built-in life insurance component, beneficiary designation overrides will
- Privacy: ownership is via insurance contract, not directly visible in share registries

**Disadvantages:**
- **High total costs** — wrapper management fee + underlying fund fees can reach 1.5%–3.0% p.a., which often **exceeds the tax deferral benefit**, especially for smaller portfolios
- **Limited investment options** — typically restricted to provider's own fund selection
- **No hankintameno-olettama** or dividend partial exemption available
- **Lock-in effect** — surrender charges discourage early withdrawal
- **Break-even period typically 10+ years** — fees must be offset by tax deferral savings

### 4.5 Suitability for €30K–€50K Earners

For middle-income investors with moderate portfolio sizes (€10,000–€50,000), **sijoitusvakuutus is generally NOT recommended** as a primary investment vehicle. The annual fees (often 0.5%–1.5% on top of fund fees) typically eat more return than the tax deferral provides. The product is more suitable for high-net-worth individuals (€100K+) with 10+ year horizons and estate planning needs.

**Better alternative:** OST (for listed shares) or low-cost index fund via arvo-osuustili.

## Section 6: ETF & Fund Taxation in Finland — Objective 6

### 6.1 Fund Taxation — General Rules

In Finland, ETFs and mutual funds (sijoitusrahastot) are taxed identically under capital income tax rules. There are two share types with fundamentally different tax timing ([Vero.fi — Sijoitusrahasto-osuudet](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/sijoitusrahastoosuude/)):

**Tuotto-osuus (Distributing shares):**
- Fund pays out income (dividends, interest) to holder — typically quarterly or annually
- Distribution is **100% taxable as capital income** in the year received
- Fund company automatically withholds 30% ennakonpidätys

**Kasvuosuus (Accumulating shares):**
- Fund reinvests all income back into the fund — no distributions
- **No annual tax** — tax only paid when selling shares (capital gains)
- Enables superior compound growth (korkoa korolle) due to tax deferral

**Switching between tuotto→kasvu within the same fund is TAX-FREE.** Switching to a different fund = deemed sale → capital gains tax applies.

### 6.2 Capital Gains on Fund Sales

When selling fund shares, capital gains are calculated identically to shares:
- **Actual cost method:** Sale price − (purchase price + acquisition costs)
- **Hankintameno-olettama:** 20% of sale price (held <10yr) or 40% (held ≥10yr)
- Taxpayer chooses whichever produces lower taxable gain
- **FIFO principle** applies by default, but for fund shares you can request specific lot liquidation by registration number
- **Loss carry-forward:** 5 years against all capital income

### 6.3 Irish UCITS ETFs — Tax Treatment in Finland

Most popular European ETFs (iShares, Vanguard, Amundi) are domiciled in Ireland as UCITS funds. Key tax implications for Finnish investors ([Nordnet — Osingot uudelleensijoittava ETF](https://www.nordnet.fi/koulu/osingot-uudelleensijoittava-etf)):

**Funds/ETFs do NOT pay corporate tax on received dividends and interest** — applies to both Finnish-domiciled and Irish-domiciled UCITS funds.

**Exception — US dividends:** EU-domiciled funds must pay **15% US withholding tax** on dividends from US companies (per Ireland-US / Finland-US tax treaties). This is unavoidable regardless of fund type.

**EU dividends:** No cross-border withholding tax within the EU — EU-domiciled funds receive 100% of EU company dividends.

### 6.4 Distributing vs. Accumulating — Tax Efficiency Comparison

| Aspect | Distributing (Tuotto-osuus) | Accumulating (Kasvuosuus) |
|--------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Dividend treatment** | Paid to investor annually | Reinvested in fund |
| **Annual tax** | 30%/34% on distributions | None |
| **Tax on sale** | 30%/34% on capital gain | 30%/34% on capital gain |
| **Compound effect** | Reduced by annual tax leakage | Full compounding |
| **US dividend drag** | 15% withholding + Finnish tax on remainder | 15% withholding only (compounding on 85%) |
| **Annual cost difference** | **+0.3%–0.9% extra tax drag** per year | Baseline |

**Worked example (from Nordnet analysis):** With a 2%–5% dividend yield assumption, the extra annual tax drag of a distributing fund vs. accumulating is:
- 2% yield × (30% − 15%) = **0.3%** per year
- 5% yield × (33% − 15%) = **0.9%** per year

Over 20 years, this compounds into a **significant difference in terminal wealth**.

### 6.5 Recommendation for Middle-Income Investors

**Always prefer accumulating (Acc/C) over distributing (Dist/D) fund shares** when investing for long-term growth. The tax deferral on reinvested dividends compounds meaningfully over time.

For Finnish ETF investors the optimal choice is:
- **Irish-domiciled accumulating UCITS ETFs** — no Finnish annual tax, only 15% withholding on US dividends (unavoidable), full compounding until sale
- **Finnish accumulating index funds** (e.g., Nordnet Suomi Indeksi, Seligson) — no foreign withholding issues for Finnish stocks

## Section 7: Dividend Taxation Optimization — Objective 7

### 7.1 Listed Finnish Company Dividends (Pörssiosinko)

When receiving dividends from a listed (pörssiyhtiö) company ([Vero.fi — Listatusta yhtiöstä saadut osingot](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/osingot/listatusta-yhti%C3%B6st%C3%A4-saadut-osingot/)):

| Component | Rate |
|-----------|------|
| **Taxable as capital income** | **85%** of dividend |
| **Tax-free** | **15%** of dividend |
| **Ennakonpidätys (withholding)** | **25.5%** of total dividend (paid by company) |

**Effective tax rates on listed dividends:**

| Capital Income Level | Calculation | Effective Dividend Tax Rate |
|---------------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| ≤€30,000 | 85% × 30% | **25.5%** |
| >€30,000 | 85% × 34% | **28.9%** |

The 25.5% ennakonpidätys exactly matches the tax due when all capital income is ≤€30,000. If total capital income exceeds €30,000 (including dividends), the taxpayer owes the difference (28.9% − 25.5% = 3.4% additional) in their tax return.

### 7.2 Worked Example (from Vero.fi)

**Aleksi receives €10,000 in listed company dividends. He has €110 in asset management fees.**
- Taxable dividend income: €10,000 × 85% = **€8,500**
- Deductible management fee: €110 − €50 (own-risk) = **€60**
- Taxable capital income: €8,500 − €60 = **€8,440**
- Tax: €8,440 × 30% = **€2,532**
- Withholding already collected: €10,000 × 25.5% = **€2,550**
- **Refund: €18** (overpaid due to management fee deduction)

### 7.3 Foreign Dividends — Tax Treaty Treatment

Foreign dividends from companies in **EU, EEA, or tax treaty countries** are taxed identically to Finnish dividends (85%/15% split for listed companies) ([Vero.fi — Ulkomailta saadut osingot](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/omaisuus/sijoitukset/osingot/ulkomailta-saadut-osingot/)):

**Key differences from domestic dividends:**
- **No Finnish ennakonpidätys** — foreign company may withhold tax per tax treaty
- **Withholding tax credit:** Foreign tax withheld is credited against Finnish tax to avoid double taxation

**Common withholding tax rates (per Finland's tax treaties):**

| Country | Treaty Rate on Listed Dividends |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| USA | 15% |
| Sweden | 15% |
| Germany | 15% (reduced from 26.375%) |
| UK | 0% (no dividend withholding) |
| Ireland | 0% (for most shares) |
| Denmark | 15% (reduced from 27%) |
| Norway | 15% |

**Note:** Treaty rate claims must be filed — default withholding is often higher (e.g., US 30% default → 15% treaty). Finnish brokers typically handle W-8BEN for US shares automatically.

### 7.4 Dividend Tax Optimization — OST vs. Arvo-osuustili

| Scenario | OST | Arvo-osuustili |
|----------|-----|----------------|
| Finnish listed dividend | **No 15% exemption** — full amount taxed on withdrawal | **15% tax-free** → effective 25.5% at ≤€30K |
| Foreign dividend (US) | Dividend enters account after 15% US withholding, compounding tax-free | 15% US withholding + 85% × Finnish tax rate |

**For dividend-focused investors:** The arvo-osuustili is more tax-efficient for Finnish listed company dividends due to the 15% tax-free portion. The OST loses this exemption, making it better suited for growth-oriented (non-dividend) strategies or frequent trading.

## Section 8: Tax Loss Harvesting (Tappioiden Vähentäminen) — Objective 8

### 8.1 Capital Loss Deduction Rules

From [Vero.fi — Omaisuuden luovutusvoitot ja -tappiot (syventävä ohje)](https://www.vero.fi/syventavat-vero-ohjeet/ohje-hakusivu/48935/omaisuuden-luovutusvoitot-ja-tappiot-luonnollisen-henkilon-tuloverotuksessa3/), effective 1.1.2026:

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Deduction priority** | First from capital gains, then from **all capital income** (incl. rental income, interest) |
| **Carry-forward** | **5 years** from the year of the loss |
| **Mandatory deduction** | Loss MUST be deducted in first year possible — taxpayer cannot defer to a preferred year |
| **Spousal transfer** | ❌ Cannot transfer losses to spouse |
| **Alijäämähyvitys** | ❌ Capital losses do NOT create alijäämähyvitys — cannot reduce earned income taxes |
| **Small sales exclusion** | If total proceeds ≤ €1,000/year AND total acquisition costs ≤ €1,000, losses are NOT deductible |
| **Multiple year losses** | Oldest year's losses deducted first (FIFO) |

### 8.2 Worked Example (from Vero.fi)

**A has a €15,000 capital loss in 2022. No capital gains that year, but €8,000 net rental income.**
- Loss deducted from rental income: €8,000
- Remaining loss: €15,000 − €8,000 = **€7,000** → carried forward (max until 2027)
- If €8,000 rental income again in 2023: remaining €7,000 deducted → only €1,000 rental income taxed

### 8.3 No Wash Sale Rule in Finland

**Finland does NOT have explicit wash sale rules** like the US (IRC §1091). This creates a significant tax planning opportunity:

- **You can sell at a loss and immediately repurchase** the same security — the loss is fully deductible
- **Same-day repurchase** is generally accepted by Verohallinto
- The new purchase creates a fresh hankintameno (acquisition cost base)
- The general anti-abuse clause (VML 28 §) exists but is practically **never applied** to ordinary securities trading

**Practical strategy — Year-end tax loss harvesting:**
1. Before December 31, identify positions with unrealized losses
2. Sell the losing positions to realize the capital loss
3. Immediately repurchase the same shares if you still want exposure
4. The loss offsets capital gains and other capital income in that tax year
5. If no capital income that year, the loss carries forward 5 years

### 8.4 Important Limitations for Tax Loss Harvesting

- **OST positions:** Selling at a loss within OST has NO tax effect — losses are only deductible when closing the entire account
- **FIFO applies:** On arvo-osuustili, you must sell the oldest lots first (per-account). This may force you to realize gains on older profitable lots before reaching the loss lots
- **FIFO workaround:** Hold the same stock at different brokers → choose which account to sell from → sell from the account where the older lots are at a loss
- **€1,000 threshold:** If all your sales in the year total ≤ €1,000 in proceeds AND acquisition costs ≤ €1,000, losses are NOT deductible
- **Transaction costs:** Brokerage fees on the sell + repurchase reduce the net benefit

### 8.5 Tax Loss Harvesting — Cost-Benefit for Middle-Income Investors

For a €40,000 earner with €5,000 unrealized loss:
- **Tax saving:** €5,000 × 30% = **€1,500** (if offset against capital income ≤€30K)
- **Transaction cost:** 2 trades × ~€7 = **€14** (at typical broker rates)
- **Net benefit: ~€1,486** — tax loss harvesting is highly profitable in Finland due to the absence of wash sale rules

## Section 11: Verovähennykset — Tax Deductions for Investors — Objective 11

### 11.1 Investment Loan Interest Deduction (Tulonhankkimisvelan korot)

Investment loan interest is **100% deductible from capital income** in Finland ([Vero.fi — Korko tuloverotuksessa](https://www.vero.fi/henkiloasiakkaat/vahennykset/mita-voi-vahentaa/velat-ja-korot/korko-tuloverotuksessa/), updated 1.1.2026):

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Deductible interest** | Only interest on **tulonhankkimisvelka** (income-producing debt) |
| **Deduction amount** | **100%** of interest from pääomatulot |
| **Timing** | Deducted in the calendar year the interest was paid |
| **Who deducts** | Only the debtor themselves; each spouse deducts own debt interest |
| **If no capital income** | Creates alijäämä → 30% alijäämähyvitys from earned income taxes |

**What qualifies as tulonhankkimisvelka (income-producing debt):**
- Loans for purchasing investment apartments (vuokra-asunto)
- Loans for purchasing shares/stocks (osakesäästäminen)
- Any borrowing whose purpose is generating taxable income

**What does NOT qualify:**
- Asuntolainan korot (home loan interest) — **NOT deductible since 2023**
- Opintolainan korot (student loan interest) — NOT deductible
- Kulutusluoton korot (consumer credit interest) — NOT deductible
- Interest on loans for tax-exempt income acquisition
- Interest on loans for lähdevero-subject interest income (e.g., Finnish bank deposits)
- Interest on voluntary individual pension insurance (vapaaehtoinen eläkevakuutus) loans

**Loan arrangement fees (liitännäiskulut):**
Järjestelypalkkio (arrangement fees), toimitusmaksu (delivery charges) etc. are **NOT counted as interest** for korkovähennys purposes. However, arrangement fees on tulonhankkimisvelka can be deducted as **tulonhankkimiskulut** (income acquisition costs) — see Section 11.2.

**Joint debt between spouses:**
- Interest deducted by the spouse who actually paid the interest
- If payer not reported → each deducts 50%
- Cannot transfer interest deduction to the other spouse's capital income (henkilökohtainen, not perhekohtainen)

### 11.1.1 Worked Example — Investment Loan Interest

**Armi takes a bank loan in February and buys a rental apartment. She rents it from March. She earns €5,000 rental income and pays €1,000 in hoitovastike and €1,500 in loan interest.**

1. Rental income reported: €5,000
2. Hoitovastike deducted from rental income: −€1,000
3. Loan interest deducted as tulonhankkimisvelan korko: −€1,500
4. **Net taxable capital income: €2,500**
5. Tax: €2,500 × 30% = **€750**

Without the interest deduction, tax would be: (€5,000 − €1,000) × 30% = €1,200 → **interest deduction saves €450**

### 11.1.2 Investment Loan Interest — Application to Stock/ETF Investing

For a middle-income investor who takes a loan specifically to invest in shares or ETFs:
- **The loan must be clearly earmarked** for investment purchases
- Bank records should show the loan amount matching investment purchases
- Interest is fully deductible from ALL capital income (not just from the specific investment's returns)
- **Practical tip:** Keep the investment loan in a separate loan account from any personal/consumer lending to maintain clear documentation

## Section 12: After-Tax ROI Comparison Matrix — Objective 12

### 12.1 Worked Example A — OST vs. Arvo-osuustili (TASK-4.5)

**Scenario:** €40,000 earner invests €20,000 in a global ETF on osakesäästötili (OST) vs. arvo-osuustili (AO). Sells after 5 years with 50% capital gain (total value €30,000, gain €10,000). No dividends (accumulating ETF).

#### Option 1: Osakesäästötili (OST)

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Investment | | €20,000 |
| Value after 5 years | €20,000 × 1.50 | €30,000 |
| **Gain** | €30,000 − €20,000 | **€10,000** |
| Withdraw full amount | €30,000 | |
| Taxable portion on withdrawal | €10,000 / €30,000 × €30,000 (proportional) | **€10,000** |
| Capital income tax (≤€30K) | €10,000 × 30% | **€3,000** |
| **Net after tax** | €30,000 − €3,000 | **€27,000** |
| **Effective tax rate on gain** | €3,000 / €10,000 | **30.0%** |

#### Option 2: Arvo-osuustili (AO) with FIFO

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Investment | | €20,000 |
| Value after 5 years | €20,000 × 1.50 | €30,000 |
| **Gain** | €30,000 − €20,000 | **€10,000** |
| Actual cost method | €30,000 − €20,000 − ~€14 fees | **€9,986** |
| Hankintameno-olettama (20%, held <10yr) | €30,000 − (20% × €30,000) = €30,000 − €6,000 | **€24,000** |
| **Best method: Actual cost** | (actual €9,986 < olettama €24,000) | **€9,986** taxable |
| Tax (≤€30K) | €9,986 × 30% | **€2,996** |
| **Net after tax** | €30,000 − €2,996 | **€27,004** |
| **Effective tax rate on gain** | €2,996 / €10,000 | **30.0%** |

#### Option 2b: AO with Hankintameno-olettama (if bought cheaply long ago — €2,000 original cost)

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Sale price | | €30,000 |
| Actual cost | €2,000 + fees | €2,014 |
| Gain (actual) | €30,000 − €2,014 | **€27,986** |
| Hankintameno-olettama (20%, <10yr) | €30,000 − €6,000 | **€24,000** |
| **Best method: Olettama** | (€24,000 < €27,986) | **€24,000** taxable |

**Verdict Example A:** For a 50% gain over 5 years, OST and AO produce nearly identical tax outcomes (~30%). The OST advantage is **no tax on dividends during holding** and **no capital gains tax on internal trades**. AO advantage is dividend 15% exemption if receiving Finnish dividends.

### 12.2 Worked Example B — Finnish Dividend Tax Calculation (TASK-4.6)

**Scenario:** €40,000 gross salary earner receives €3,000 in Finnish listed company dividends.

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Gross dividends received | | €3,000 |
| Taxable portion (85%) | €3,000 × 85% | **€2,550** |
| Tax-free portion (15%) | €3,000 × 15% | €450 |
| Other capital income | Assume none | €0 |
| Total taxable capital income | | **€2,550** |

### 12.3 Worked Example C — Foreign ETF Sale with Hankintameno-olettama (TASK-4.7)

**Scenario:** Sell iShares Core MSCI World ETF on arvo-osuustili with €5,000 gain after 12 years of holding. Original investment €8,000, sale price €13,000.

#### Method 1: Actual Cost (FIFO)

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Sale price | | €13,000 |
| Purchase price | | €8,000 |
| Brokerage fees (buy + sell) | ~€14 | €14 |
| **Taxable gain** | €13,000 − €8,000 − €14 | **€4,986** |
| Tax (≤€30K) | €4,986 × 30% | **€1,496** |

#### Method 2: Hankintameno-olettama 40% (held ≥10 years)

| Step | Calculation | Amount |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Sale price | | €13,000 |
| Hankintameno-olettama (40%) | 40% × €13,000 | €5,200 |
| **Taxable gain** | €13,000 − €5,200 | **€7,800** |
| Tax (≤€30K) | €7,800 × 30% | **€2,340** |

**Best method: Actual cost** → taxable gain €4,986 < olettama €7,800 → use actual cost → tax **€1,496**

#### Method 2 wins if original cost is very low:
The olettama 40% is better when: actual purchase price < 40% × sale price = €5,200. So if you bought for **less than €5,200**, the olettama saves tax.

**Example recalculated with €3,000 original cost (333% return):**
- Actual cost: €13,000 − €3,000 = €10,000 taxable → tax €3,000
- Olettama 40%: €13,000 − €5,200 = €7,800 taxable → tax €2,340
- **Olettama saves €660** in this case

## Section 13: Step-by-Step Tax-Optimized Investment Framework — Objective 13

### 13.1 Who Is This For?

Finnish resident, age 25–55, employed with gross salary €30,000–€50,000, looking to build long-term wealth through tax-efficient investing. No prior investment accounts or a small existing portfolio.

### 13.2 The Framework — 7 Steps

#### Step 1: Open an Osakesäästötili (OST) — Priority: HIGH

**Where:** Nordnet (€0 fees, widest market access, most popular)
- Deposit up to **€100,000** lifetime ceiling (deposits only — gains don't count)
- Start with monthly deposits of €200–€500
- **Buy accumulating global ETF:** Invesco FTSE All-World (TER 0.15%) or iShares Core MSCI World (TER 0.20%)
- **Tax advantage:** No tax on dividends, capital gains, or internal trades until withdrawal
- **Best for:** Long-term growth investing (5+ years), no Finnish dividend stocks

#### Step 2: Open an Arvo-osuustili (AO) — For Overflow & Finnish Dividends

**Where:** Same broker as OST (Nordnet recommended) or OP for existing customers
- Use AO for investments that **don't fit in OST**: mutual funds, Finnish dividend stocks (which get the 15% tax-free dividend treatment on AO but not on OST)
- **Buy:** Nordnet Indeksirahasto Suomi (0% fee) for Finnish market exposure
- **Tax advantage:** €1,000/year tax-free capital gains threshold, 15% dividend exemption, hankintameno-olettama 40% after 10 years
- **Best for:** Finnish dividend stocks (Fortum, Nordea, UPM etc.) and Finnish index funds

#### Step 3: Contribute €5,000 to PS-tili — In 2026 ONLY

**CRITICAL — 2026 is the LAST YEAR for the tax deduction:**
- Contribute **€5,000** to an existing PS-tili (or vapaaehtoinen eläkevakuutus if you have one)
- Immediate tax benefit: **€1,500** (at 30% rate) or **€1,700** (at 34% rate)
- If you don't have a PS-tili, evaluate whether the lock-in until age 68-70 is acceptable
- **After 2026:** Do NOT contribute — no tax benefit, severe lock-in, punitive death taxation

#### Step 4: Establish Monthly Automatic Investing (Kuukausisäästäminen)

Set up automatic monthly purchases to eliminate market timing:

| Priority | Account | Product | Monthly Amount | Annual Cost |
|----------|---------|---------|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | OST (Nordnet) | Global ETF (Acc) | €200–€400 | ~€0 custody + €7/trade |
| 2 | AO (Nordnet) | Nordnet Suomi Indeksi | €50–€100 | **€0** |
| 3 | AO (Nordnet/OP) | Additional global ETF | As available | TER only |

**Target allocation:** 80% global developed markets + 20% Finnish/Nordic = diversified core portfolio

#### Step 5: Execute Year-End Tax Loss Harvesting (Every December)

Before December 31 each year:
1. **Review all AO positions** (not OST — losses in OST have no tax effect)
2. **Identify positions with unrealized losses** — even €500 losses are worth harvesting
3. **Sell the losing positions** to realize capital losses
4. **Immediately repurchase** the same securities — Finland has NO wash sale rule
5. **Calculate benefit:** €5,000 loss × 30% = **€1,500 tax saved** (minus ~€14 brokerage)
6. Losses offset capital gains AND other capital income (rental, interest, dividends)
7. Unused losses carry forward **5 years** — mandatory deduction in first available year

#### Step 6: Claim All Available Tax Deductions (Every Spring)

When filing your veroilmoitus (by April/May):
1. **Verify bank-reported loan interest** — ensure tulonhankkimisvelka is classified correctly
2. **Check custody/management fee deduction** — banks report automatically, but verify
3. **Claim PS-tili deduction** (if contributed in 2026)
4. **Consider työhuonevähennys** — only if you actively work on investments from home (not passive holding)
5. **Review alijäämähyvitys** — if capital income losses/deductions exceed capital income, 30% becomes credit against earned income tax (max €1,400 + €400–€800 child supplement)

#### Step 7: Review Account Strategy Annually

Annual checklist:
- [ ] **OST approaching €100K ceiling?** → Start directing new money to AO
- [ ] **AO positions held >10 years?** → Hankintameno-olettama 40% now available — factor into sell decisions
- [ ] **Capital income approaching €30K?** → Consider deferring some sales to next year to stay in 30% bracket
- [ ] **Broker fee tier upgraded?** → Check if accumulated commissions qualify you for lower rates
- [ ] **Life changes?** → Marriage, children, home purchase may change optimal strategy (e.g., child supplement for alijäämähyvitys)
