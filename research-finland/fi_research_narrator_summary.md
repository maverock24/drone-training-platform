Welcome to this guide on building wealth through smart investing in Finland. This summary is designed for anyone living in Finland who earns a moderate salary, roughly between thirty thousand and fifty thousand euros per year, and wants to make their money grow in the most tax-efficient way possible. Everything in this guide is based on the tax rules and investment options available in the year two thousand and twenty-six. Even if you have never invested before, this guide will walk you through the key concepts in plain, simple language.

Let us start with the most important thing to understand: how Finland taxes the money you earn from investments.

In Finland, the money you make from your job, called earned income, is taxed separately from the money you make from investments, which is called capital income. Capital income includes things like profit from selling shares, dividends you receive from companies you own shares in, interest from savings accounts, and rental income from property. The tax rate on capital income is thirty percent on the first thirty thousand euros you earn from investments in a year. If your investment income goes above thirty thousand euros, the rate on the portion above that threshold increases to thirty-four percent. These rates have not changed for two thousand and twenty-six.

To put that in perspective, if you earned thirty-five thousand euros from investments in a single year, you would pay thirty percent on the first thirty thousand, which equals nine thousand euros, and thirty-four percent on the remaining five thousand, which equals one thousand seven hundred euros. Your total tax would be ten thousand seven hundred euros. This is actually a favorable rate compared to the tax on your salary, which for someone earning between thirty and fifty thousand euros from their job typically falls between thirty-five and forty-five percent when you add up all the government contributions and municipal taxes.

Now let us talk about the different types of accounts you can use to invest in Finland, because the type of account you choose can make a huge difference in how much tax you end up paying.

The most powerful tool for a middle-income investor in Finland is something called the osakesäästötili, which translates to equity savings account. Think of it as a special container for your stock investments. The magic of this account is that while your money is inside it, you pay absolutely no tax. You can buy and sell shares freely, receive dividends, and reinvest them, all without triggering any tax. You only pay tax when you take money out of the account. The maximum amount you can deposit into this account over your lifetime is one hundred thousand euros. This limit was raised from fifty thousand euros at the beginning of two thousand and twenty-four, making it even more useful. One important restriction is that you can only hold listed shares in this account, meaning stocks that are traded on a stock exchange. You cannot put mutual funds or bonds in it. You are also limited to having just one of these accounts at a time.

When you do withdraw money from the equity savings account, the tax works proportionally. That means if your account is worth fifteen thousand euros and five thousand of that is profit, then one-third of every withdrawal is considered taxable profit. If you take out one thousand five hundred euros, five hundred of it would be taxed and one thousand would be your original money coming back tax-free.

The second type of account is called an arvo-osuustili, which is a regular securities account, sometimes called a book-entry account. This is the standard investment account that everyone can open. Unlike the equity savings account, you pay taxes as events happen. When you sell a stock for a profit, you pay tax on that profit right away. When you receive dividends from Finnish companies, you pay tax on those as well. However, this account has some advantages the equity savings account does not have.

First, when you receive dividends from Finnish listed companies through a regular securities account, fifteen percent of the dividend is completely tax-free. Only eighty-five percent is taxed. This means the effective tax rate on dividends in this account is about twenty-five and a half percent, compared to thirty percent through the equity savings account. So if you plan to invest mainly in Finnish companies that pay good dividends, the regular account can actually be more tax-efficient.

Second, the regular account offers something called the acquisition cost presumption, known in Finnish as hankintameno-olettama. This is a special rule that can reduce your tax when selling investments you have held for a long time. If you have held a stock for less than ten years, you can assume that your purchase price was twenty percent of the selling price, even if you actually paid less. If you held it for ten years or longer, you can assume your purchase price was forty percent of the selling price. You always get to choose whichever method gives you the lower tax. This can save you a lot of money on old investments that have grown significantly in value.

Third, if your total sales proceeds for the entire year are one thousand euros or less, any gains are completely tax-free. This is a small but nice benefit for people just starting out.

There is a third type of investment wrapper called a sijoitusvakuutus, which translates to investment insurance. This is essentially an insurance policy that wraps around your investments. Like the equity savings account, you can trade freely inside it without paying tax until you withdraw money. However, the problem is the fees. These insurance wrappers typically charge between half a percent and one percent per year on top of the fees charged by the funds inside them. For a middle-income investor with a modest portfolio, these fees usually eat up more money than you save in tax deferral. For this reason, we do not recommend investment insurance as a primary investment tool for people in the thirty to fifty thousand euro income range.

So to summarize the account types: the equity savings account is best for long-term stock investing where you want to buy, sell, and reinvest without tax drag. The regular securities account is better for Finnish dividend stocks and for very long-term holdings where the acquisition cost presumption becomes valuable. Investment insurance is generally too expensive for middle-income investors.

Let us now turn to an important topic: choosing the right investment products.

When it comes to what you should actually invest in, the evidence strongly favors low-cost index funds and exchange-traded funds, commonly called ETFs. An index fund simply tries to match the performance of a broad market, like all the major companies in the world, rather than trying to pick individual winners. The key advantage is cost. The annual management fee, measured by something called the total expense ratio, is extremely low for index funds compared to actively managed funds.

The cheapest global investment fund currently available to Finnish investors is the Invesco FTSE All-World fund, with an annual cost of just zero point one five percent. Another excellent option is the iShares Core MSCI World fund at zero point two zero percent. Compare these to a typical Finnish fund company like Seligson, whose index funds cost around zero point four four to zero point six four percent per year, and you can see the difference.

This difference might sound small, but it compounds dramatically over time. On a twenty thousand euro investment over twenty years, the difference between a zero point one five percent fund and a zero point six four percent fund amounts to roughly five thousand to ten thousand euros in lost returns. That is real money.

There is another important choice to make when selecting funds: whether to choose an accumulating fund or a distributing fund. An accumulating fund takes any dividends it receives and automatically reinvests them back into the fund. A distributing fund pays those dividends out to you in cash. For tax purposes in Finland, the accumulating version is almost always better. When dividends are paid out to you, you must pay tax on them immediately, which creates a drag of roughly zero point three to zero point nine percent per year. With an accumulating fund, those dividends stay inside the fund and keep growing without any tax being taken, until you eventually sell. Over twenty years, this tax deferral makes a meaningful difference to your final wealth.

Most of the popular European ETFs are set up in Ireland as something called UCITS funds. The reason Ireland is popular is that Irish-domiciled funds benefit from favorable tax treaties with many countries.  However, there is one unavoidable drag: when these funds receive dividends from American companies, the United States withholds fifteen percent of the dividend at source. This applies regardless of fund type and cannot be avoided. But within the European Union, dividends flow between countries without any withholding tax on dividends between companies, which is good news.

So the recommendation is clear: choose an accumulating, Irish-domiciled global ETF for your core investment portfolio.

Now let us discuss how dividends are taxed when you receive them directly on a regular securities account.

When a Finnish listed company pays you a dividend, eighty-five percent of it is taxable as capital income and fifteen percent is completely tax-free. The company automatically withholds twenty-five and a half percent of the total dividend for tax purposes. If all your capital income for the year stays below thirty thousand euros, that withholding exactly matches your tax bill and you owe nothing extra. If your capital income exceeds thirty thousand euros, you will owe a small additional amount because the rate on the excess is thirty-four percent instead of thirty.

For dividends from foreign companies, the rules are similar if the company is in a country that has a tax treaty with Finland. However, the foreign country may also withhold tax from the dividend. For example, the United States withholds fifteen percent, Sweden withholds fifteen percent, and Germany withholds fifteen percent under their respective tax treaties with Finland. Finland gives you credit for this foreign tax so you do not pay tax twice on the same income.

Here is an important practical point: if you hold Finnish dividend stocks inside the equity savings account, you lose the fifteen percent tax-free portion. Dividends received inside that account are fully taxed when you withdraw. This means Finnish dividend stocks are actually better held in a regular securities account. Reserve the equity savings account for growth-oriented investments where dividends are less important.

Let us now cover one of the most valuable tax strategies available to Finnish investors: tax loss harvesting.

Sometimes your investments lose value. While that is never pleasant, in Finland you can at least use those losses to reduce your tax bill. When you sell an investment at a loss, that loss can be deducted from your capital gains and even from other capital income like rental income. If you do not have enough capital income to use up the loss, you can carry it forward for five years.

What makes Finland particularly special in this regard is that there is no so-called wash sale rule. In some countries, like the United States, if you sell a stock at a loss and buy the same stock back within thirty days, the loss is disallowed for tax purposes. Finland has no such restriction. You can sell a stock at a loss today and buy it back immediately, the very same day, and the loss is still fully deductible. This is perfectly legal and widely practiced.

The practical strategy is simple. Every year, before the end of December, look through your regular securities account for any positions that are currently worth less than what you paid for them. Sell those positions to realize the loss for tax purposes. Then, if you still want to own those investments, simply buy them back right away. The total cost of doing this is just two trading fees, typically around seven euros each, so about fourteen euros total. But if you have, say, five thousand euros in unrealized losses, realizing them saves you five thousand times thirty percent, which equals one thousand five hundred euros in taxes. That is an enormous return for fourteen euros in transaction costs.

One important caveat: this strategy only works on the regular securities account. Losses realized inside the equity savings account have no tax effect unless you close the entire account.

Now, there is one more time-sensitive opportunity to discuss: voluntary pension savings.

Finland has a voluntary pension savings system called the PS-tili, which stands for pitkäaikaissäästötili, or long-term savings account. You can contribute up to five thousand euros per year, and that contribution is deducted from your taxable capital income. At a thirty percent tax rate, a five thousand euro contribution saves you one thousand five hundred euros in taxes immediately. That is like getting a thirty percent instant return on your money.

However, and this is crucial, the Finnish government has confirmed that this tax deduction will be completely removed starting on the first of January, two thousand and twenty-seven. This means two thousand and twenty-six is the very last year you can benefit from this deduction. There is no grandfathering, meaning even people who already have these accounts will lose the deduction.

The money in a pension savings account is locked away until you reach the age of sixty-eight to seventy, depending on when you were born, and then must be withdrawn gradually over at least ten years. All withdrawals are fully taxed as capital income. There is also an unfortunate double-taxation problem if you pass away while still holding the account: the full amount is first taxed as capital income to your estate, and whatever remains is then subject to inheritance tax.

Given the severe restrictions, our recommendation is this: if you already have a pension savings account and are comfortable with the lock-in, it may be worth making one final five thousand euro contribution in two thousand and twenty-six to capture the last one thousand five hundred euro tax benefit. After that, do not contribute any more. The equity savings account provides similar tax deferral benefits without the lock-in.

Let us now talk about choosing a broker, which is the company that gives you access to the stock market.

There are four main brokers serving Finnish retail investors: Nordnet, Nordea, OP, and Danske Bank. There is also S-Pankki, but they only offer mutual funds, not direct stock trading, so they are less relevant for this discussion.

Nordnet is the leading online-only broker in Finland and generally the best choice for passive index investors. They charge no custody fees at all, offer their own index funds with zero management fees, and have competitive trading commissions starting from three euros per trade for active traders. For a typical investor making one to three trades per month, each trade costs about seven euros.

Nordea, one of Finland's largest banks, has a unique advantage: a one percent commission cap on trades on Nordic exchanges and all ETFs. This means if you buy shares for one hundred euros, the commission is only one euro instead of the eight euro minimum that would normally apply. This makes Nordea the cheapest option for people who like to make small, frequent purchases.

OP, which stands for Osuuspankki and is Finland's largest cooperative bank, offers competitive pricing especially for existing customers who benefit from their loyalty bonus system. They return about zero point two five percent annually on fund and insurance investments to their owner-customers.

Danske Bank stands out for its member pricing programs. If you are a member of certain Finnish organizations like Akavan jäsenliitto or Suomen Osakesäästäjät, you can get exceptionally low rates: as low as zero point zero five percent commission with a minimum of just two euros. Danske Bank is also the only broker that offers automatic monthly stock savings on the equity savings account with no minimum fee, which is great for consistent small investments.

For most middle-income investors starting out, Nordnet is the recommended first choice due to its zero custody fees, free index funds, and straightforward pricing.

Now let us discuss tax deductions that investors can claim.

If you take out a loan specifically to invest, the interest you pay on that loan is one hundred percent deductible from your capital income. This is called tulonhankkimisvelan korko in Finnish, meaning interest on income-producing debt. The key requirement is that the loan must be clearly used for investment purposes, such as buying shares or a rental apartment. Personal loans, home loans, and student loans are not deductible. If your interest costs exceed your capital income, the excess creates a deficit that can reduce your earned income taxes by up to thirty percent of the deficit amount, with a maximum benefit of one thousand four hundred euros per year, plus an additional four hundred to eight hundred euros if you have children.

You can also deduct custody and management fees charged by your broker or fund company, but there is a fifty euro annual threshold that is not deductible. In practice, most middle-income investors have such low custody fees that this deduction does not amount to much.

There is also a home office deduction available for investment activity, but it only applies if you actively work on your investments from home on a regular basis. Simply holding stocks passively and making occasional trades does not qualify.

Let us now put all of this together and look at what these different strategies actually produce over time.

If you invest twenty thousand euros in a global exchange-traded fund through the equity savings account and leave it for twenty years, assuming a seven percent average annual return, your money would grow to approximately seventy-seven thousand euros before taxes. When you withdraw, you would pay about seventeen thousand euros in capital income tax, leaving you with approximately sixty thousand euros. That is three times your original investment.

Compare this to simply leaving twenty thousand euros in a bank savings account earning one and a half percent interest. After twenty years, with interest taxed at thirty percent each year, you would have only about twenty-five thousand euros. The difference between investing and saving is roughly thirty-five thousand euros, which represents a hundred and forty percent advantage.

Now, the difference between the equity savings account and the regular securities account is actually quite small for accumulating funds where no dividends are distributed during the holding period. Both produce similar after-tax results of around sixty thousand euros. The biggest factor is not which account you use, but rather the cost of the fund you invest in and the power of compound growth over time.

An investment insurance wrapper, due to its additional half percent or more in annual fees, would produce only about fifty-one thousand euros over the same period. That is nearly ten thousand euros less than the direct approach, which clearly shows why we do not recommend investment insurance for middle-income investors.

So, what should you actually do? Here is a simple, step-by-step plan.

Step one: Open an equity savings account, ideally at Nordnet. Start depositing money regularly, even if it is just two hundred or three hundred euros per month. Buy an accumulating global exchange-traded fund, such as the Invesco FTSE All-World fund with its zero point one five percent annual cost, or the iShares Core MSCI World fund at zero point two zero percent.

Step two: If you also want exposure to Finnish stocks, particularly dividend-paying companies, open a regular securities account at the same broker. Consider the Nordnet Finland index fund, which has essentially zero management fees.

Step three: If it is still two thousand and twenty-six and you have a pension savings account, make a final contribution of up to five thousand euros to capture the last year of the tax deduction. That gives you an immediate one thousand five hundred euro tax benefit. Do not contribute after two thousand and twenty-six.

Step four: Set up automatic monthly investments. Consistency matters more than timing. Investing a fixed amount every month, regardless of market conditions, is one of the most proven strategies for building wealth over time.

Step five: Every December, review your regular securities account for investment positions that have lost value. Sell them to realize the losses for tax purposes, and if you still believe in those investments, buy them back immediately. Remember, Finland allows this with no restrictions. Even a modest five thousand euro loss saves you one thousand five hundred euros in taxes for a cost of about fourteen euros in trading fees.

Step six: Each spring, when filing your tax return, make sure your investment loan interest is correctly classified, check that any custody fees are reported, and claim any other deductions you are entitled to.

Step seven: Review your strategy once a year. As your equity savings account approaches the one hundred thousand euro deposit ceiling, start directing new money to the regular securities account. Keep an eye on whether your total capital income might exceed thirty thousand euros in any given year, because above that threshold the tax rate jumps from thirty to thirty-four percent. If you can defer some sales to the following year to stay below that line, it can save you money.

There are some common mistakes to avoid. Do not buy distributing fund classes when accumulating versions are available, as the annual tax drag is significant over time. Do not hold Finnish dividend stocks in the equity savings account when the regular account gives you the fifteen percent exemption. Do not pay for expensive actively managed funds when index funds consistently perform just as well or better at a fraction of the cost. Do not contribute to the pension savings account after two thousand and twenty-six since the tax benefit is being removed. And do not forget to harvest your tax losses each year, because leaving realized losses on the table is essentially giving the tax office money you could have kept.

Let us close with the key takeaways.

Investing in Finland as a middle-income earner is genuinely rewarding when done thoughtfully. The tax system, while complex, offers several generous tools: the tax-deferred equity savings account, the fifteen percent dividend exemption on the regular account, the acquisition cost presumption for long-held investments, and the freedom to harvest tax losses without any wash sale restrictions. By choosing cheap index funds, using the right account for the right investment, and taking advantage of every legitimate tax deduction, a person investing three hundred euros per month can realistically build one hundred and twenty-five thousand to one hundred and thirty thousand euros in net wealth over twenty years. That is a life-changing amount of money, and it starts with the simple decision to begin.

Tax laws and fees can change, so always check the current rules before making investment decisions.
